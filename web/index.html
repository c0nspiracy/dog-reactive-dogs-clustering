What cluster does your dog reactive dog (DRD) fall into?

This is an owner-friendly screening tool helps you estimate your dog’s likely cluster type based on the frequency of nine key reactive behaviours.

It provides some basic guidance on what this means for risk and where to focus training.

Please note this tool does not replace professional behavioural assessment or advice, and we recommend consulting a veterinary behaviourist or clinical animal behaviourist before making management or training decisions.

The research

  • A dog-reactive dog was defined as a dog exhibiting one or more of the following nine behaviours in response to another dog: barking, growling, snarling (raised lip), lunging, snapping, nipping, biting, whining, and a stiff posture with raised hackles accompanied by intense staring.
  • Our research shows that DRDs can be grouped into one of 4 clusters reflecting different styles of responding, shaped by rearing environment, motivation, learning history, and thresholds for reactivity.
  • Knowing your dog’s cluster can help you better understand their risk of escalation and where to focus risk management and training to reduce the associated risk of harm.
  • Professional behavioural support is strongly recommended for dog reactive dogs, especially those that fall into clusters 3 or 4.

Understanding Your Dog’s Cluster

Cluster 1: Low risk signallers

Profile: Moderate frustration and posturing, low oral attack. These dogs tend to avoid escalation and may prefer avoidance.

Focus: Teach and reinforce alternative communication signals e.g., turning away, looking back towards owner. Reward avoidance behaviours to build confidence in safer responses.

Cluster 2: Frustrated escalators

Profile: High frustration, moderate-to-high posturing, moderate oral attack. Dogs in this group are more likely to escalate when arousal levels are high, this may be more likely if they are restrained e.g., on a lead.

Focus: Work on counterconditioning i.e. teaching an alternative response to other dogs and desensitisation i.e. gradual exposure to reduce arousal.

Cluster 3: Impulsive escalators

Profile: Moderate frustration, but high posturing and oral attack. These dogs are more willing to make physical contact, even without high frustration. This may relate to traits such as impulsivity, poor risk assessment, or reinforcement history.

Focus: Prioritise risk management by maximising distance from other dogs and ensuring proper walking equipment. Long-term work may include addressing impulsivity, and consistent reinforcement of appropriate behaviours when seeing other dogs.

Cluster 4: Risky rapid escalators

Profile: Very high frustration, posturing, and oral attack. These dogs escalate rapidly and represent the highest risk group.

Focus: Immediate risk management is essential by avoiding situations that trigger reactivity and employing strict safety protocols. We suggest professional assessment and treatment for this group.
